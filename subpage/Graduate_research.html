Graduate Research

In Pugh Lab, Cornell University
 
 

01

Translational and rotational setting of nucleosomes across a human genome
Abstract

Understanding how transcription factors (TFs) access DNA within chromatin is central to gene regulation. TFs recognize their DNA binding site either when it is nucleosome-free or its helical site is rotationally accessible on a nucleosome surface. These are manifested as translational and rotational phasing, which are largely undefined in human cells. We developed Benzonase-seq to measure genome-wide phasing and enhanced ChIP-exo (v6) to assess phasing on DNA bound by TFs, including transient ones. We show that phasing differs between bound and unbound sites. For example, unbound CTCF sites are encased within DNA-encoded rotationally phased nucleosomes. But there is no consensus translational phasing until CTCF binds. Similar relationships exist with other TFs like ZKSCAN1, and for transiently binding TFs like FoxA and NFIA. Our approach also refines nucleosome maps near promoters and reveals subnucleosomal structures. These findings offer new insights into how TFs interact with nucleosomes to shape the chromatin landscape.

 
 

02

An integrated genome-wide mechanism of RNA polymerase II initiation complex assembly
Abstract

How transcription pre-initiation complexes (PICs) assemble on natural promoters remains poorly understood. Here we propose a molecular mechanism governing PIC assembly at human promoters genome-wide that integrates transcription start sites, TATA boxes, +1 nucleosomes, and transcription factor binding. We find that +1 nucleosomes have robust DNA-encoded rotational phasing. This, in conjunction with TFIID (TAF3) interactions and activator (e.g., SP1, GABPA and NFYC) interactions with TFIID (TAF4) and TFIIA concentrate and orient the transcription machinery within DNA gyres. Such placement positions RNA polymerase (Pol) II to conduct a tightly focused search for the optimal initiator dinucleotide. Pol II then initiates transcription and pauses at the +1 nucleosome without disrupting its rotational phase unless the nucleosome is unstable. Together, these findings define key organizational steps that govern PIC assembly, transcription initiation, and pausing within the natural chromatin landscape of promoters.

 
 

03

Single cell ChIP-exo by split-pool barcoding
Abstract

Most methods that study the landscape of TF binding including ChIP-exo assay measure the ensemble TF signals across millions of cells. Though protein-DNA interactions with single-bp precision can be achieved currently, it is still obscure critical information about the epigenome information of any given cell. In this research, we combine nuclei barcoding with ChIP-exo assay to profile TF binding at base-bp resolution as well as single cell resolution. Here in insoluble nuclei, the fragmented chromatins are lambda exonuclease digested and each cell gets a unique barcode by nuclei split-pool. After pooling barcoded nuclei for immunoprecipitation, the sequence information will demultiplex the TF profiling at single-cell resolution.
